# Gnomoria Player Feedback Research

## Research Complete

Comprehensive player feedback has been gathered from Steam discussions, community forums, game reviews, and comparison threads. The findings are compiled below, organized by theme to directly inform Ingnomia development priorities.

---

# WHAT PLAYERS LOVED

## 1. Accessibility as a Gateway to the Genre

The single most praised aspect of Gnomoria across all sources. Players consistently described it as the game that made the DF-like genre approachable.

- "For anyone put off by DF but in love with the concept, the first game I would point you to is Gnomoria." (Quarter to Three Forums)
- "A great entry point for this particular brand of ultra-detailed sim, with enough features and polish to keep you digging for hours." (Gold-Plated Games review)
- "At the basic/beginning stages, Gnomoria is easier to get into than DF because of its simple interface." (Quarter to Three Forums)
- "When Gnomoria came out, it really scratched an itch that no other game could scratch." (Steam discussion)

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: This is the core identity to preserve. Accessibility without dumbing down. The isometric pixel art, point-and-click GUI, and gradual complexity reveal were the key ingredients.

## 2. The Ant-Farm Experience / Building Freedom

Players loved watching their settlements grow and having total freedom in construction.

- "The ant-farm style gameplay is engaging at its very core." (UNconstant review)
- "You are given impressive reign to tunnel and build as you see fit, and sprawling underground kingdoms are not out of the question." (Gold-Plated Games)
- "Anything you see in-game is breakable and also repurposed to suit your needs."
- Z-levels were repeatedly cited as a major advantage over RimWorld. Building both underground and upward was a beloved feature.
- Vertical efficiency: "A gnome doesn't count the vertical steps it takes to get something" -- no penalty for vertical builds.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Z-levels are a core differentiator. RimWorld still lacks them. Lean into vertical building as a flagship feature.

## 3. The Economy and Crafting System

The production chain system was widely praised as deep yet learnable.

- "Where this game truly shines is the depth. The economy is deep and varied, but based on a common and simple set of principles."
- "One of the most well-thought-out and funny crafting and building systems."
- Material variety was appreciated: "There is a lot of variety already in the visual distinctions between using different wood and stone."
- The forge-to-machine-shop-to-engineer pipeline was called out as particularly satisfying.
- Kingdom Worth as a progression driver (attracting merchants, gnomads, and enemies) was a clever unifying mechanic.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Keep the material-matters philosophy. Every material having visual distinction and value differences is important. Kingdom Worth as the central progression metric should be preserved and expanded.

## 4. The Tinkering/Engineering System

The gradual unlock of mechanical devices through research was a unique and beloved feature.

- "The tinkering and engineering being basically hidden and locked out at start really helped make the game feel less overwhelming."
- Players enjoyed the research-driven progression: discovering springs, gears, rods, traps, and mechanical walls over time.
- Creative trap design was a major late-game draw -- mechanical walls, hatches, pistons, pressure plates.
- This system was cited as something that "one-ups Dwarf Fortress in some respects."

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: The tinkering system is a signature feature. Expand it significantly -- more devices, more complex contraptions, more reasons to engineer.

## 5. Difficulty Scaling Through Kingdom Worth

The wealth-based difficulty system was praised as elegant.

- As settlement wealth increases, stronger enemies attack, better merchants arrive, and more gnomads show up.
- Great Hall multiplier added strategic depth -- building a lavish hall was a calculated risk/reward.
- Difficulty sliders at world generation (map size, flatness, resource depth, enemy types) gave good replayability.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: This organic difficulty curve is worth preserving and deepening.

## 6. Visual Charm and Audio

- The pixel art aesthetic was consistently praised: "The textures and music are all well-done and have a nice charming quality."
- Players valued seeing material differences visually (different wood types, stone types, metals).
- The isometric perspective was preferred over flat 2D by many players.
- "The menu music is giving players some heavy nostalgia."

# WHAT PLAYERS WANTED MORE OF

## 1. Late-Game Content and Escalation (MOST REQUESTED)

This was the single most common complaint about depth. Multiple sources independently identified this gap.

- "Once you understand the mechanics, learn how to start... the game will become similar to Minecraft where once you have become established, you are basically scraping for goals."
- "Beyond a certain point, neither your population will grow nor the enemy attacks gain much strength."
- "At extreme late game, even a 40-gnome army with steel equipment struggles against nightly waves. It's a slow death for your kingdom as your army gets whittled away."
- Players wanted: wall-breaking enemies, tunnel-digging creatures, more complex threats, meaningful content beyond survival.
- The lack of biome diversity was noted: "Players cannot progress to challenging environments like deserts or tundra."

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Late-game content is the #1 gap to fill. Need escalating threats, new enemy types with unique mechanics, mega-threats, and goals beyond "survive and get richer."

## 2. Deeper Combat System

Combat was functional but shallow.

- "Combat is better than 'best armoured gnomes TANK RUSH'" was the minimum bar players wanted.
- Balance issues: trained soldiers in steel could still lose to basic goblins due to tuning problems.
- Players wanted: varied weapon utility (hammers vs specific enemies), ranged weapon improvements, squad tactics, flying enemies, wall-breaching enemies.
- Specific requests: spears (melee + throwing), slings, bows, halberds, cannons, flaming crossbow bolts.
- Ranged weapons couldn't shoot targets one block above/below -- a frustrating limitation.
- Civilians would charge into combat suicidally: "Your farmer gnome can be out picking strawberries and--when a goblin with a claymore wanders into view--the gnome's immediate response is to charge into battle."

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Combat needs significant depth. Weapon types should matter tactically. Civilian AI must avoid combat zones. Squad tactics and positioning should be meaningful.

## 3. Gnome Personality and Character Depth

Players wanted gnomes to feel like individuals, not interchangeable drones.

- "The gnomes do feel quite like drones, and there have been plans on fleshing out the personality in the future."
- Gnomoria lacked DF's personality system, moods, personal biographies, and relationship dynamics.
- Players requested: weddings, children, personal relations, happiness systems, inspirations.
- RimWorld's character intimacy was cited as something to aspire to: "much more intimacy and detail."
- The needs system (food/drink/sleep) was basic -- players wanted psychological needs, aspirations, and character-driven stories.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Gnome personality is a major opportunity. Adding relationships, moods, personal goals, and narrative events would differentiate from the original dramatically.

## 4. A Wider World

- "The only drawback for Gnomoria was that it didn't have the wider world aspect that DF has. If it did then G would destroy DF in all aspects."
- Players wanted: exploration beyond the map, sending expeditions, diplomatic interaction with other kingdoms, caravans, a world map.
- RimWorld's world map with factions was cited as a feature Gnomoria lacked entirely.
- Specific requests: military expeditions against goblin kingdoms, settler colonization (establish new kingdoms), diplomatic delegates.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: A wider world system (even abstracted) would be a major feature add. Sending parties out, discovering locations, raiding, trading with distant settlements.

## 5. Better Tutorial and Onboarding

- "It's a disjointing tumble into the start of the game and there's not a lot out there to get players grounded."
- "A tutorial walking you through the foundations of production would be a godsend."
- "Players literally have to spend hours fumbling around until they figure out on their own how to play."
- The wiki was sparse and sometimes outdated.
- Complex production chains (like making a bed requiring 4 workshops) were not explained.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Progressive tutorials or guided first-game scenarios are essential. Tooltips on everything. Contextual hints.

## 6. Magic System

- Multiple sources mention magic as a desired feature.
- The original Ingnomia developer had this planned.
- Players suggested: rare items at deep levels enabling magic, magical crafting, enchantments.

## 7. Multiplayer

- Repeatedly mentioned as a dream feature for the genre.
- "No colony management game offered multiplayer support."
- Settletopia was literally built to fill this gap.

## 8. Random Events and Narrative

- Players wanted: natural disasters, animal stampedes, procedural quests with rewards, festivals celebrating victories.
- The game lacked RimWorld's storyteller/event system that creates emergent narratives.

## 9. Better Mod Support

- "Rimworld is more fleshed out and looks like it was designed with mod support from the beginning, compared to Gnomoria's mod system, which feels like an afterthought."
- Only ~3 mods on Nexus, limited Steam Workshop content.
- Mods that did exist included: difficulty tweaks, decorative floors, auto-trading, glassworks.

# GAMEPLAY PAIN POINTS (NOT DEV-ABANDONMENT)

## 1. Performance Degradation (CRITICAL)

The most universally cited technical issue.

- "Gnomoria lagged pretty badly at mid game or later."
- "The game tends to chug a bit" and becomes "laggy and difficult to play" when kingdoms grow.
- Pathfinding was the primary culprit: "Lag is nearly always caused by pathing."
- Beetle spawning could cascade into hundreds of creatures, bringing the game to a slideshow.
- Memory leak suspected: FPS dropping significantly after 1-2 hours.
- "There is huge lag, even on pause, when a gnome cannot reach his destination."
- Game was single-threaded; CPU core speed was everything.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Performance at scale is existential. Pathfinding optimization, entity caps, and multi-threaded simulation are must-haves. This was the #1 reason people stopped playing.

## 2. Critical Bugs

- **Wheelbarrow/container bug**: Gnomes get stuck carrying items permanently, eventually starving to death.
- **Beetle cascade**: Beetles multiply endlessly, destroying performance.
- **Merchant trading bug**: Items not properly appearing after trades.
- **Item ghosting**: Items visible on ground but unable to be picked up.
- **Exhaustion death**: Gnomes falling asleep while carrying items, becoming permanently stuck.
- **Material selection**: Complex recipes ignoring material preferences ("If you order a golden mechanical wall, they will still use steel for some parts").

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Robust item/container system is critical. Material selection in crafting must work correctly. Entity lifecycle management must prevent cascading bugs.

## 3. AI Behavior Issues

- "Gnomes don't sleep when they need to, don't eat when they need to, and will literally kill themselves doing the most stupid stunts."
- Suicidal combat behavior: civilians charging armed enemies.
- Priority system for groves didn't work properly.
- Gnomes would ignore critical needs until too late.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Gnome AI needs a proper needs-based priority system with safety behaviors. Civilians must flee danger. Priority settings must actually work.

## 4. Isometric Camera Frustrations

- "The isometric camera causes issues as one must constantly rotate the camera to see items in shadows."
- "Trying to dig stairs and ramps in the chunky isometric interface" was problematic.
- Precisely aligning constructions required excessive effort.

**Takeaway for Ingnomia**: Camera controls and construction placement need to be smooth and intuitive. Consider transparency/cutaway options for occluded areas.

## 5. UI Friction

- Over-use of sub-menus.
- Rooms not functioning with no feedback about why.
- No tooltips in many places.
- Material selection in crafting was cumbersome.
- Lack of build order queue at kingdom-wide scale.

# COMPETITIVE COMPARISON SUMMARY

## What Gnomoria Did BETTER Than Dwarf Fortress
- Visual presentation and readability
- Mouse-driven GUI with sub-menus instead of keyboard shortcuts
- Lower learning curve / faster to get started
- Tinkering/engineering research system
- Guns (blunderbusses, flintlocks) without mods
- Difficulty customization sliders

## What Gnomoria Did BETTER Than RimWorld
- Z-levels (vertical building/mining)
- Larger population counts
- More crafting categories
- Fantasy aesthetic with underground kingdoms
- Material variety with visual distinction

## What Gnomoria Did WORSE Than Dwarf Fortress
- Far less depth in every system
- No wider world / adventure mode
- No gnome personalities, moods, or biographies
- No wall carvings, artifacts, or legendary crafting
- Fewer enemy types and threat variety
- No water physics (rivers, waterfalls)
- Less biome diversity

## What Gnomoria Did WORSE Than RimWorld
- No storyteller/event system for emergent narrative
- Less character depth and relationships
- Weaker mod support
- No world map or caravan system
- No faction diplomacy
- Harder to learn (despite being easier than DF)
- More bugs, worse performance at scale

# REPEATED THEMES (BY FREQUENCY)

These themes appeared independently across 3+ sources, indicating real community consensus:

1. **Performance at scale** -- mentioned in virtually every discussion about late-game
2. **Late-game content drought** -- mentioned in every review and comparison thread
3. **Accessibility is the core appeal** -- unanimous across all sources
4. **Z-levels are a killer feature** -- always mentioned in RimWorld comparisons
5. **Combat needs depth** -- mentioned in reviews, feature requests, and comparisons
6. **Gnomes need personality** -- repeated across Steam, forums, and feature request threads
7. **Need a wider world** -- mentioned in DF comparisons and feature requests
8. **Tutorial/onboarding is inadequate** -- mentioned in every review
9. **Material variety matters** -- praised in reviews, wanted deeper in feature requests
10. **Tinkering/engineering is a signature feature** -- praised and wanted expanded everywhere

---

# PRIORITY RECOMMENDATIONS FOR INGNOMIA

Based on this research, ordered by impact:

### Must-Have (what made Gnomoria loved)
1. Smooth, accessible UI with tooltips and progressive disclosure
2. Z-level building (up and down) with intuitive camera
3. Deep crafting/production chains with material variety
4. Kingdom Worth-driven progression and difficulty scaling
5. Tinkering/engineering research system (expanded)
6. Solid performance at 50+ gnomes

### High Priority (what players wanted most)
7. Late-game content: escalating threats, mega-events, goals beyond survival
8. Deeper combat: weapon types matter, squad tactics, civilian safety AI
9. Gnome personality: relationships, moods, aspirations, personal stories
10. Wider world: expeditions, diplomacy, external threats/opportunities
11. Tutorial/onboarding system
12. Random events and narrative generation

### Nice to Have (frequently requested)
13. Magic system
14. Better mod support / moddable data
15. Multiplayer (dream feature but enormous scope)
16. Biome diversity
17. Water physics improvements
