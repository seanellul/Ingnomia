# Game Identity & Worldbuilding

> **Working pitch:** *"The world went dark. Build the last light. Hold it."*

---

## 1. Core Fantasy

You are the last light in a world consumed by darkness. Your settlement is a beacon — a medieval stronghold of stone, fire, and defiance against an encroaching supernatural night. By day, you build. By night, you defend. The deeper you dig, the closer you get to the source of the darkness — and the chance to end it.

This is not a monastery game. This is a **fortress-building, stone-laying, fire-keeping survival colony sim** set in a medieval fantasy world that has fallen to an ancient evil. Your people are survivors — soldiers, masons, farmers, priests, smiths — building humanity's last stand.

## 2. Tone & Aesthetic References

| Reference | What we take from it |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Don't Starve** | The oppressive night, the importance of light/fire as survival mechanic, the constant dread |
| **Dwarf Fortress** | Depth of simulation, digging deep, hubris-driven failure states, emergent stories |
| **Rimworld** | Colony management, migrant system, escalating threats, the endgame "escape" objective |
| **Dark Souls** | The fading fire, linking the flame, the weight of a dying world — thematic DNA |
| **Darkest Dungeon** | Stress, madness, the cost of fighting the dark — morale as a mechanic |
| **Bastion** | Rebuilding after catastrophe, the feeling of reclaiming ground piece by piece |

**Tone:** Cozy-dark. The warmth of a hearthfire matters *because* of what's outside. Candlelit workshops, stone walls against the fog, gallows humor around a campfire. Not grimdark — *determined*. The feeling of building something that matters against impossible odds.

## 4. Thematic Pillars

### 4.1 Light vs. Darkness
The fundamental axis of the game. Light is territory, safety, civilization. Darkness is the unknown, the enemy, the wild. Your controlled space is literally your *lit* space.

- Light sources project influence: torches (small), hearths (medium), bonfires (large), lighthouses/beacons (massive)
- Beyond the light: fog, shadows, creatures, corruption
- Light degrades — fires must be fed, torches burn out, oil lamps need refilling
- The constant maintenance of light IS the survival loop

### 4.2 Masonry & Building
Stone is king. Building with stone is the primary progression path. Walls, towers, keeps, cathedrals, fortifications — the act of *building in stone* is what separates you from the darkness.

- Wood structures are temporary, vulnerable, early-game
- Stone is permanent, defensible, impressive — mid to late game
- Advanced masonry unlocks architectural features: arches, buttresses, vaulted ceilings, battlements
- **Castle-building should feel aspirational** — players should *want* to build grand structures
- Freemasonry nods: mason's marks on stones, guild halls, secret knowledge unlocked through mastery, architectural "mysteries" (trade secrets that unlock advanced building techniques)
- Historical references: Gothic cathedrals, crusader castles, Romanesque monasteries

### 4.3 Medieval Fantasy Setting
Grounded medieval with restrained fantasy elements. Not high fantasy — no elves, no flashy magic. Think:
- Stone and iron, not mithril and enchanted weapons
- Prayers and blessings, not fireballs
- Herbalism and alchemy, not potion shops
- The supernatural is *terrifying*, not commonplace

### 4.4 The Descent (Endgame)
Digging down is progression toward the endgame. Each depth layer reveals more about what happened to the world and gets closer to the Source of darkness.

- Upper layers: natural caves, old mines, ruins of the civilization that fell
- Middle layers: ancient structures, warnings left by those who came before
- Deep layers: wrongness — geometry that doesn't make sense, whispers, corruption
- The Source: the final objective. Reaching and destroying it triggers the endgame siege — the darkness throws everything at you as you attempt to unmake it

## 5. Key Mechanics

### 5.1 Day/Night Cycle
The signature mechanic. No other colony sim owns this the way we should.

- **Day:** Full productivity. Build, farm, craft, mine, explore, trade. Safe within your light perimeter.
- **Dusk:** Warning phase. Workers return to safety. Final preparations. Anxiety builds.
- **Night:** Threats emerge. Creatures probe your defenses. Citizens who are caught outside are in mortal danger. Soldiers man the walls. Priests tend the sacred fires. The settlement hunkers down.
- **Dawn:** Relief. Survey the damage. Repair. Expand. Push the perimeter further.

Night intensity should escalate over time and based on how deep you've dug / how much attention you've drawn.

### 5.2 Hope & Migration
Colonists don't come because you're wealthy — they come because you're *alive*.

- **Hope** is a settlement-wide stat driven by: population alive, buildings standing, light coverage, food stores, morale, victories against the dark
- Higher hope = more frequent migrants, better-skilled arrivals, rare visitors (traders, scholars, knights)
- Low hope = fewer arrivals, people may leave, despair events
- Migrants arrive by road, by boat, or simply emerge from the wilderness — drawn to your light
- Each migrant has a story: "fled when my village fell", "heard there was still light in the north", "the order sent me"

### 5.3 Professions & Society
A full medieval settlement, not just one archetype:

- **Masons** — the backbone. Build in stone. Unlock advanced architecture.
- **Soldiers/Guards** — man the walls, patrol the perimeter, fight the dark
- **Priests/Clerics** — maintain sacred sites, bless the settlement, boost morale, perform rites
- **Farmers** — food production, animal husbandry, herbalism
- **Miners** — dig deeper, extract resources, discover what's below
- **Smiths** — weapons, tools, armor, metalwork
- **Woodworkers** — early-game building, furniture, siege equipment
- **Scholars** — research, translate ancient texts found below, unlock knowledge
- **Alchemists** — potions, light-enhancing compounds, fire mixtures

### 5.4 Religion & the Sacred
Religion is a *system*, not the identity. One pillar among many.

- Monasteries, chapels, shrines are special buildings that project hope and light
- Priests are one profession — important but not dominant
- Religious rites can bless ground (making it resist corruption), boost morale, or provide temporary buffs
- Freemasonry angle: the mason's guild has its own "sacred geometry" — architectural secrets that function like a parallel knowledge system to religion. Builders and priests both fight the dark, but through different means (stone vs. prayer)

### 5.5 The Darkness
What's out there? This needs heavy worldbuilding, but initial direction:

- **Not just undead.** The darkness *corrupts*. Animals become wrong. Plants wither or twist. The land itself turns hostile.
- **Escalating threat types:**
  - Early: shadow creatures, corrupted wildlife, eerie phenomena
  - Mid: organized attacks, cursed weather (darkness storms), corruption spreading toward light sources
  - Late: named horrors, siege-level assaults, the ground fighting back as you dig
  - Endgame: everything at once
- **The darkness is intelligent** — or at least directed. It probes, it adapts, it remembers where you're weak.

## 6. Setting & Lore (Rough)

### What happened?
The world fell. An ancient evil — sealed away long ago — broke free. (Or was released. Or was always there, held back by something that finally failed.) The darkness spread from a single point and consumed civilization over weeks/months. Cities fell. Armies crumbled. Most of the world is now lost.

### Who are your people?
Refugees. Survivors. The last free people of a fallen kingdom. Mixed backgrounds — soldiers who lost their units, farmers who lost their land, priests who lost their temples, scholars who lost their libraries. United by one thing: they're still alive, and they intend to stay that way.

Medieval fantasy humans by default. Other fantasy peoples (if any) should be restrained — maybe dwarven refugees who lost *their* mountain, adding masonry expertise. But humans should be the core.

### Where are you?
A remote, defensible location — a rocky coastline, a mountain valley, a plateau. Somewhere the darkness hasn't fully reached *yet*. Chosen not because it's ideal, but because it's *possible*.
